Date1928
DescriptionDay dresses of the twenties preserve a neat, business-like simplicity in its tubular, extremely slender shape, as described by C.W. Cunnington. Female breasts and waists are obliterated in a boyish shape. But in the later half of the decade, there are suggestions of a softening, especially in the skirt that begins to flare loosely, freed from straight, precise pleats. This sports dress shows just such a flare in the skirt that is made of 3 panels. Two of the panels are semi-circular; they join at the center front on the straight grain, and finish at the side back, stitched to the side back panel that is all on the straight grain. This construction places a bias curve on the sides that leaves plenty of room for any sports activities. Madelyn Shaw, Guest Curator of the exhibit, Silk in New England Society, 1730-1930, here at the Smith College Museum of Art, wrote further about the material: By 1920, the American silk industry was the largest in the world, consuming more raw silk from Japan and China than all of Europe put together. Constrained by the shortages of wool and cotton during World War I, American silk manufacturers developed new silk and silk-mixed fabrics to encourage American women to wear silk around the clock and across the seasons. Sports silks, like sportswear, were truly American inventions and were exported to markets in Europe and South America. two-piece tan silk tweed sports dress trimmed with silk ribbon
